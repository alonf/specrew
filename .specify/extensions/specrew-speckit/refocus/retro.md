## Retro-stage discipline

1. **Four sections, all substantive.** what-went-well / what-was-hard / lessons-learned / signals-for-next-iteration. A retro that only celebrates is ceremonial; the hard parts are the payload.
2. **Lessons get owners and next actions.** A lesson without a named follow-up (a chore, a proposal candidate, a charter edit, a validator rule) rolls into the next iteration invisibly — exactly what the no-gap policy forbids.
3. **Calibrate against actuals.** Compare estimated vs actual effort per task; feed the variance into capacity calibration. Don't explain variance away — record it.
4. **Triage reviewer-instruction candidates.** Anything review caught (or missed) that a better playbook would have caught: PROMOTE to durable methodology, DEFER with reason, or DROP explicitly.
5. **Friction is data.** Tooling defects, gate false-positives, redeploy drift, scaffold overwrites — file them as candidates with enough context to slice later; don't just endure them.
6. **Stop at the boundary.** retro → iteration-closeout is human-judgment-required. Commit retro.md, packet, wait.

Known traps: retro written from memory instead of the drift-log/journal/commits; lessons repeated verbatim from last iteration (the lesson didn't stick — that IS the finding); skipping calibration because the iteration "felt fine".
